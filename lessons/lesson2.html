What is a Qubit?

The fundamental unit of quantum information and how it differs from classical bits

From Bits to Qubits

In classical computing, information is stored in bits. A bit is the smallest unit of information and can be in one of two states: 0 or 1.

Everything in your computer ‚Äî text, images, videos, programs ‚Äî is ultimately represented as a sequence of 0s and 1s. This binary system is simple, reliable, and forms the foundation of all modern computing.

Enter the Qubit

But here's the revolutionary difference: a qubit can also be in a superposition of both states simultaneously.

Physical Implementations

Qubits can be implemented using various physical systems:

  • Superconducting circuits: Used by IBM and Google
  • Trapped ions: Individual atoms held by electromagnetic fields
  • Photons: Particles of light with different polarizations
  • Topological qubits: Microsoft's approach using exotic quasiparticles
  • Quantum dots: Semiconductor nanostructures

Why Qubits Matter

The power of quantum computing comes from having many qubits working together:

  • 2 qubits can be in a superposition of 4 states (00, 01, 10, 11)
  • 3 qubits can be in a superposition of 8 states
  • n qubits can be in a superposition of 2‚Åø states simultaneously!

This exponential scaling is what gives quantum computers their potential advantage for certain problems.
